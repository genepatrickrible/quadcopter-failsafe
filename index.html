About this work

Stable flight normally needs at least four propellers, yet a quadcopter that loses a motor mid-air does not have to fall. This body of work, carried out at the Electrical and Electronics Engineering Institute of the University of the Philippines Diliman, shows how to keep one flying and land it safely after the complete loss of two opposing or three motors, using only its onboard sensors and processor.

The two papers build on each other. The first establishes a complete dynamic model of the quadcopter using alternative, low-cost methods: it recovers propeller thrust, torque, and speed relationships, and the airframe's rotational aerodynamic drag, without the expensive reaction-torque sensor such characterization usually requires. The second uses that model to design and test two fail-safe controller architectures (three-propeller and two-propeller) that detect a failure, identify which motors are gone, and stabilize the vehicle for a controlled return and landing. The fail-safe paper received the Best Presentation Award at the 2020 6th International Conference on Control, Automation and Robotics (ICCAR) in Singapore.

Fail-Safe Controller Architectures for Quadcopter with Motor Failures

Gene Patrick S. Rible, Nicolette Ann A. Arriola, Manuel C. Ramos Jr.

2020 6th Int'l Conf. on Control, Automation and Robotics (ICCAR), Singapore 2020  Best Presentation Award

A fail-safe algorithm that stabilizes and lands a quadcopter after the complete loss of two opposing or three motors. Both the three-propeller and two-propeller architectures pair an LQR inner attitude controller with a damped second-order outer controller, run entirely on the onboard processor, and were validated in simulation and on hardware.

Accurate quadcopter control needs the relationships between motor command, propeller speed, thrust, and reaction torque, plus the airframe's moments of inertia and aerodynamic drag. These are normally measured with costly equipment such as a reaction-torque sensor. The journal paper replaces that with simple benchtop methods: a weighing scale and tachometer for thrust and speed, current and voltage readings for electrical torque, a pendulum swing for the moments of inertia, and a free-spinning rope-and-bearing rig for the body drag. Scaling motor commands to a finer internal range reduced thrust discretization by two orders of magnitude, giving noticeably smoother flight.

The assembled model may not capture every true parameter value, yet because the reaction torque and drag enter the dynamics in the same way, the model behaves identically to the real quadcopter. A basic PID flight controller tuned on the model held altitude and tracked roll, pitch, and yaw commands in MATLAB simulation, then flew on the physical DJI FlameWheel 450 with matching behavior. That agreement is what makes the model trustworthy as the basis for the harder fail-safe problem.

Two ways to survive a dead motor

Implemented two-propeller attitude response while hovering on the physical quadcopter

When a motor fails, the quadcopter gives up control of one attitude direction and spins about a primary axis to stay aloft. Two architectures handle this. The three-propeller case augments the inner controller with a PID on propeller-force deviations and distributes thrust across the remaining motors, lowering the demand on each. The two-propeller case decouples altitude onto its own PID controller, separate from the LQR attitude loop, which makes it more efficient, more robust, and more stable. Both were tuned and tested, with the two-propeller controller stabilizing and hovering the physical quadcopter on a rope-and-bearing rig.

The largest initial deviation each architecture can recover from. The two-propeller limits are far wider (it tolerates roll and pitch past ±90°). The highlighted yaw-rate limits on r are the binding constraints: the quadcopter must already be spinning fast enough about its primary axis.

Each combination of failed motors produces a distinct pattern of spikes across the angular rate and attitude readings, so the controller can identify which motors are gone from the IMU alone, with no extra sensors. Analysis of the output limits, initial-condition limits, and controller frequencies showed the two-propeller architecture tolerates far larger disturbances. A key finding: increasing the induced yaw rate unconditionally lowers the in-plane components that must be controlled, so faster spin yields greater stability. That also means larger, heavier vehicles or those with more air resistance are harder to recover.
